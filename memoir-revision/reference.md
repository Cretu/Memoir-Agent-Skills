# Revision Reference

## Purpose
Strengthen drafted chapters and verify they meet the memoir's responsibilities — without
overwriting the writer's voice. Revision is where a memoir is actually made; drafting just
gets the clay on the wheel.

## Principles

### Do
- Work big-to-small: structure → scene → sentence → responsibility.
- Cut before you add. Most memoir drafts are too long and over-explained.
- Show edits as `before → after` with a short reason.
- Treat fairness, accuracy, and safety as non-negotiable checks, not style notes.

### Don't
- Rewrite the chapter in your own voice.
- Mix passes (don't polish sentences in a scene you're recommending to cut).
- Apply changes silently — always propose and let the writer choose.

## The Four Passes (detail)

### Pass 1 — Developmental
Questions to ask of the whole chapter:
- What is this chapter *about* beyond what happens in it?
- Does it deliver the Narrative Goal from the outline?
- Which moment is the real scene? Is it given room, or rushed past?
- What is summary that pretends to be scene (or vice versa)?
- What can be cut entirely without loss?

### Pass 2 — Line editing
Common memoir fixes:
- **Telling → showing**: replace stated emotion with the observable detail that caused it.
- **Cut throat-clearing**: "I remember…", "It's funny, but…", "I'll never forget…".
- **Kill clichés and abstractions**: "a whirlwind of emotions", "life-changing".
- **Trust the reader**: delete the sentence that explains the scene you just wrote.
- **Vary rhythm**: a string of equal-length sentences flattens; break it.

### Pass 3 — Continuity
Track across the whole manuscript, not one chapter:
- Facts: ages, dates, names, places, spellings.
- Timeline order and any unexplained jumps.
- Narrator distance and tense (per the style guide).
- Repeated anecdotes or images used without intent.

### Pass 4 — Responsibility
Pull from `memoir-ethics-and-care.md`:
- **Accuracy** (Part 3): no invented-as-fact events; honest about reconstructed dialogue
  and uncertain detail.
- **Fairness** (Part 2): real people rendered as full humans; privacy choices applied
  consistently.
- **Safety** (Part 1): trauma handled with only the detail the writer chose to include.
- **Disclosure**: log changed names / composites / compressed timelines for the
  author's note.

## The Read-Aloud Test
The single most effective revision tool. Prose that is hard to read aloud is hard to
read at all. Encourage the writer to read a passage out loud (or read it back to them):
- Where you stumble, the sentence is tangled.
- Where you run out of breath, it's too long.
- Where it sounds like a stranger, the voice has slipped.

## Front Matter (end of project)
When all chapters pass, help assemble:
- **Title** (and subtitle — often the "so what" stated plainly).
- **Dedication** (optional, one line).
- **Author's note** — built from `chapters/authors_note_flags.md`: changed names,
  composites, reconstructed dialogue, compressed time. A short, honest note here is what
  keeps the truth contract intact.

## Knowing When to Stop
Revision can become avoidance. Define "done" against the **Project Compass**, not against
perfection — a family memoir is done when it preserves what it set out to preserve, not
when no sentence could ever be improved.
