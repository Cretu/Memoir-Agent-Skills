# Memoir Revision Examples

## Example 1: Developmental Pass (Pass 1)

**Input**: A drafted chapter where the narrator spends three paragraphs summarizing a
whole summer, then two sentences on the afternoon their parents announced the divorce.

**Editor Response**:

*Developmental pass — one structural note, most important first:*

The chapter's center of gravity is in the wrong place. Three paragraphs of summer summary
("we swam, we biked, the days were long") are doing the work of a single line — and the
real scene, the divorce announcement, is over in two sentences. The outline's Narrative
Goal is *"the moment childhood certainty broke."* Right now we race past that moment.

**Suggestion**: Compress the summer to two or three sentences of texture, and expand the
kitchen announcement into the actual scene — where everyone was sitting, who spoke first,
what you did with your hands. Let the chapter slow down exactly where it currently speeds
up.

*Shall I mark cuts in the summer section before we touch any sentences?*

## Example 2: Line Editing Pass (Pass 2)

**Input**:
> I remember that I was absolutely devastated when I found out. It was a whirlwind of
> emotions and I felt like my whole world was falling apart around me in that moment.

**Editor Response** (`before → after` with reasons):

- `I remember that I was absolutely devastated` → *(cut "I remember that"; cut
  "absolutely")* — throat-clearing and an intensifier that weakens the word it props up.
- `a whirlwind of emotions` → **delete** — cliché and an abstraction; it tells us nothing
  specific.
- `my whole world was falling apart around me` → replace with the observable detail.

**Proposed**:
> I read the letter twice. Then I put it back in the envelope, lined the torn edge up as
> if that mattered, and sat on the stairs until the hall went dark.

*This shows the devastation through what the hands do, and lets you keep the feeling
unstated. Want this, or your own version of the same idea?*

## Example 3: Continuity Pass (Pass 3)

**Editor Response**:

*Continuity pass — three things to reconcile across chapters:*

1. **Age mismatch**: Chapter 1 says you were *seven* when you moved to the coast;
   Chapter 4 says the move happened when you were *nine*. Which is right?
2. **Name**: Your aunt is "Aunt Vera" in Chapter 2 and "Aunt Vira" in Chapter 6.
3. **Repeated image**: The "screen door banging" appears as a key beat in both Chapter 3
   and Chapter 5. Powerful once — is the echo intentional, or should one go?

*These don't change your story; they protect the reader's trust in it.*

## Example 4: Responsibility Pass (Pass 4)

**Input**: A chapter that names the narrator's living ex-spouse and describes them, in the
narrator's words, as "a liar who never loved anyone but himself."

**Editor Response**:

*Responsibility pass — two flags to decide on purpose, not by accident
(see `memoir-ethics-and-care.md`):*

1. **Fairness (Part 2)**: "a liar who never loved anyone but himself" states his inner
   nature as fact. It will read as fairer — and land harder — if it stays *your
   experience*: what he did and said, and what it cost you, without claiming to know his
   heart. Consider showing one concrete broken promise instead of the verdict.
2. **Privacy / disclosure**: He's living and named. Your Project Compass says *"some names
   changed."* If you want to keep that here, I'll change the name and log it for the
   author's note. If you keep the real name, that's a deliberate choice worth making
   eyes-open.

*I've added a draft line to `chapters/authors_note_flags.md`: "Some names and identifying
details have been changed." Adjust as you like.*

## Example 5: The Read-Aloud Test

**Input**:
> Walking down the long winding road that led from the house where I had grown up and
> spent the entirety of my childhood toward the station where the train that would take
> me away was already waiting, I felt a feeling of profound and overwhelming sadness.

**Editor Response**:

Try reading that aloud — you'll run out of breath before the train. It's one 45-word
sentence carrying four ideas. Breaking it lets each land:

> The road from the house wound down to the station. I had grown up here; I had never
> left. The train was already waiting. I walked toward it and did not look back.

*Same content, four sentences, room to breathe. The short last sentence does the work
"profound and overwhelming sadness" was trying to do — without naming the feeling at all.*

## Revision Checklist (all passes)

- [ ] **Developmental**: chapter delivers its Narrative Goal; the real scene gets room
- [ ] **Developmental**: theme present, not just events
- [ ] **Line**: telling replaced with showing; throat-clearing and clichés cut
- [ ] **Line**: sentence rhythm varied; passes the read-aloud test
- [ ] **Continuity**: ages, dates, names, places consistent across chapters
- [ ] **Continuity**: tense and narrator distance match the style guide
- [ ] **Responsibility**: nothing invented-as-fact; uncertainty named honestly
- [ ] **Responsibility**: real people rendered fairly; privacy choices applied
- [ ] **Responsibility**: disclosures logged for the author's note
