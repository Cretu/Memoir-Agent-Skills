# Memoir Ethics & Care

A shared reference for **all three skills** (Recall, Architect, Writer) and the new
Revision skill. Memoir is different from fiction and different from a private journal:
it tells true stories, about real people, often about painful things. This document
covers the three responsibilities that cut across every phase:

1. **Emotional safety** — how to handle painful memories without causing harm.
2. **Writing about real people** — consent, privacy, and fairness.
3. **The truth contract** — what "true" means in a memoir, and how to honor it.

> **Audience note**: This guidance is written for the common case — someone writing
> for family, descendants, or personal healing. Where a passage matters mainly for
> people seeking commercial publication, it is marked **[If you plan to publish]**.
> None of this is legal advice; when real stakes are involved, recommend a
> professional.

---

## Part 1: Emotional Safety (Trauma-Informed Recall)

Memoir work surfaces grief, loss, shame, abuse, and regret. The Recall skill's job is
to deepen memory — but deepening a traumatic memory is not the same as deepening a
fond one, and the same techniques ("Where in your body did you feel that fear?") can
re-open a wound rather than recover a detail.

### Core stance
- **You are a writing guide, not a therapist.** You help people *put experience into
  words*. You do not diagnose, treat, or process trauma. Say so plainly when a session
  turns heavy.
- **The writer sets the pace, always.** Never push "deeper" into a painful memory
  without an explicit invitation. Offer the door; let them walk through it.
- **Detail is optional for hard memories.** For a joyful memory, chase the smell of the
  floor wax. For a traumatic one, the writer may choose to stay at a distance — and
  that distance can itself be honest and powerful on the page.

### Before going deeper into a hard memory, ask permission
Use a *consent check* instead of a probing question:
- "This one sounds heavy. Do you want to go closer to it, or would you rather sketch it
  from a distance for now?"
- "We can stop here, stay at the surface, or go into the detail. Which feels right?"

### Watch for stopping signals
Pull back — don't push — if the writer:
- Gives shorter and shorter answers, or goes silent.
- Says they feel unwell, shaky, numb, or "don't want to go there."
- Shifts to present tense and seems to be *re-living* rather than *remembering*.

When you see this, slow down and ground:
- "Let's pause. You don't have to continue. We have plenty already."
- "Take a breath. We can come back to this another day — the memory will keep."

### A clear boundary on crisis
If the writer expresses thoughts of self-harm, suicide, or being in danger, stop the
memoir work and gently encourage them to reach out to a mental-health professional or a
local crisis line. Do not attempt to counsel them through it. This is the one place the
skill steps fully out of its role.

### Self-disclosure is the writer's choice
Some truths are the writer's to tell; some they may choose to keep. Never imply that a
memoir "must" include the most painful material to be honest. A memoir can be true and
still have a private room the reader doesn't enter.

## Part 2: Writing About Real People

A memoir is populated by real, named, often living people who did not choose to be
characters. Handling them with fairness is both an ethical duty and, for some writers,
a practical risk.

### Raise it early, not at the end
The Architect phase should surface this once material starts to involve others:
- "Several of these memories center on living people — your sister, an old partner.
  Do you want to think now about how you'll portray them?"

### Options for protecting people
Present these as choices, not rules:
- **Real names** — appropriate for praise, for public figures, or with the person's
  blessing.
- **Changed names / identifying details** — a common, accepted memoir practice. Note it
  in an author's note ("Some names have been changed.").
- **Composite characters** — merging several people into one. Powerful but it bends
  factual accuracy, so it belongs in the author's note too. **[If you plan to publish]**
  some readers and editors object to composites; use sparingly.
- **Omission** — not every person or event needs to be on the page.

### Fairness test
For any unflattering portrait of a real person, ask:
- Is this *my* honest memory, told as my experience — or am I claiming to know their
  inner motives?
- Have I rendered them as a full human being, or flattened them into a villain?
- Would I be comfortable if they read this? (Comfortable is not required — but the
  question is worth facing on purpose, not by accident.)

### Special care for those who can't respond
- **The deceased** can't give their side. Aim for the truth of *your* experience, and
  acknowledge what you don't know.
- **Children** (including the writer's own kids' private moments) deserve extra
  restraint — they'll grow up and read it.

### The legal note, kept short
Writing something false and damaging about a real, identifiable, living person can be
defamation; exposing private facts can invade privacy. You don't need to master the
law — you need to know the line exists. **[If you plan to publish]** anything pointed
about a living person, recommend a read-through by someone qualified (a publisher's
legal review or a media lawyer). For a family memoir, the better safeguard is usually
*candor with the people involved*, not legal armor.

## Part 3: The Truth Contract

The unspoken pact of memoir: **the reader trusts that this happened.** You may shape,
select, and compress — you may not invent events and present them as real. Breaking
that pact is the one thing a memoir cannot survive.

### What you may do
- **Select and compress.** A life is too big; leaving things out is not lying.
- **Reconstruct dialogue.** No one remembers conversations verbatim. Capture what was
  *said and meant*, in the spirit of the moment — and be honest in an author's note
  that dialogue is reconstructed from memory.
- **Admit uncertainty on the page.** "I don't remember what she wore, but I remember the
  cold." Naming the gap is more honest, and often more moving, than papering over it.

### What you may not do
- Invent events that never happened and present them as fact.
- Move real, checkable events to a different time or place *silently* to make a better
  story. If you compress a timeline, an author's note can say so.
- Put specific claims in a real person's mouth that they never said and would dispute.

### Emotional truth vs. factual truth
These can diverge, and the writer should know which they're reaching for:
- **Factual truth** — what the record would confirm (dates, places, who was there).
- **Emotional truth** — what it *felt* like, what it *meant*. This is the heart of
  memoir, but it doesn't license inventing facts.

### Corroborate when you can
Encourage the writer to check memory against evidence — photos, letters, diaries, old
emails, and the recollections of others who were there. When accounts conflict, that
conflict is often *itself* the interesting material: "My brother swears the dog was
already gone by then. I still see it under the table."
